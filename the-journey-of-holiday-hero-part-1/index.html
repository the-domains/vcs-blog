The Journey Of Holiday Hero: Part 1

Hello everyone, this is Nick and welcome to the very first Virtual Chill Studios Developer Log! Yaaaaaay!

For our first Dev Log, I wanted for us to get to know each other a little better first. We are Virtual Chill Studios, a central Florida virtual reality game company and a two man dynamic duo of awesomeness consisting of myself (Nick) and James.

Alright, now let’s dig in. This week, we’re going to look at the development of our first VR game project for the HTC Vive, a game called Holiday Hero. This game was our first project together, cooked up in 3 very long weeks.

The game is simple: shoot snowmen with a freaking sweet machine gun while dodging an intense onslaught of incoming snowballs. We originally came up with the idea while James and I were having a conversation at the Orlando VR Meetup where the Vive was being showcased. We had both hoped we would see hundreds of little independent developers all showing off or testing out their new ideas, but that just wasn’t the case. Being mid-October, we were sure we’d see two or three different holiday-themed games but there wasn’t a single one. However, we were still pleasantly surprised by the games we did see, and especially the people we met, including the developers behind the kickass VR dojo archery game Holopoint, and they’re a fellow Floridians!

So, now we had our own game idea, we just needed to actually make it. We set to work shortly after, James working on the code and engine interactions while I worked on modeling Blender and building the map in Unity. It all started with this simple mushroom from a fantastic beginner Blender tutorial that I highly recommend.

This is a terrible screenshot of my unfinished mushroom. I made this after making the exact replica of the mushroom from the tutorial. If you’ve never used Blender before but always wanted to, do yourself a favor and download it now and make this neat little thing. It makes you feel good to have a completed product and the work itself is simple enough that my 12 year old stepson completed it over the course of 4 days. This will probably take you 4 hours, maybe a little more because the tutorial is slightly outdated.

With this mushroom, I understood how the tool worked and what I might actually be able to contribute. This was incredibly important because I had a baseline for how long it would take to do a simple model. The next morning, I started working on the game logo based on an image I had in my head on the day we first discussed the project. I needed to make a Christmas-style slightly elongated lightbulb. Can’t be that hard, right? So, I dove right in.

Aaaaannd I screwed up completely because I was guessing instead of planning. I’d learned all about loop cuts, and extruding, I knew what I was doing damnit it! I spent the first two hours just trying to figure out how to loop cut at an angle and specifically the angle I wanted, which I was being unusually picky about for some reason that day. I’d made this ridge and slope for the screw portion of the bottom of a lightbulb, but it kept breaking because I kept forgetting the shortcut keys and was missing vertices on highlight. It was moments after the above screenshot was taken that I realized I had spent the whole day in the side view of this 3d model, and I didn’t realize that what I had mapped out in my head would have ended up looking like this:

And nothing like one of these:

But I learned something! I learned that I need to really think things through before trying to model them, like, I dunno, perhaps googling for a tutorial for a second or something. But, I was still too proud. I made a freaking sweet bendy mushroom that was nothing like the tutorial one so that meant I knew my stuff.

So, I was just gonna take a break from the complexity of a lightbulb and make the simple Christmas tree I saw in my head, something like this, but less crappy:

That is when I began the long process of discovering how UV’s are not fun at all and are literally an entire art form unto themselves. After almost 9 hours of messing with UV’s to try and get my simple logo tree to look halfway decent, the tree trunk bottom still overlapped with the tree top cone, smearing brown over the green seam and green over the brown seam.

All was not lost, however, because I could still use this crummy asset I had made. Spin the tree around and while it looks simple, and has more green on the trunk, after a certain distance, you quite literally couldn’t make out that level of detail. It was one of the reasons I thought of very simple art style for the game logo to begin with: I knew it would be at a distance and only briefly noted by the player at all. This was my next lesson: good enough is good enough. Now that I had my first model, and I knew more of the keyboard shortcuts by instinct, I tackled the light bulb again and got a decent result within another 8 hours of work.

This is when I began to learn about how Blender works for rendering objects clear and how to make them emit light, and I once again had to tackle UV’s. I probably spent another 10 hours just fighting with this lightbulb because I didn’t understand how to merge objects and how merged objects change the way the shape is rendered. But, I finally came out with a great looking bulb. I googled around for some creative commons licensed fonts and learned how to get them into Blender, learned hard lessons about importing multiple blender files together, and soon I had the logo.

There it is, from mushroom to lightbulb, to cone-y Christmas tree, and finally into a decent looking game logo.
