The Journey of Holiday Hero Part 2

Hello and welcome to Part 2 of our very first VR game dev log! If you didn’t catch Part One, click here to check it out!

Alright, now let’s dive into some nitty gritty here. In Part 1 we had spent most of our time in Blender creating the assets that we needed for the game. Of course, since we are using Unity for our games currently, we had access to the asset store and found most of the holiday-themed assets we need for this project.

The problem was, I didn’t know how to use Unity any more than I’d known how to use Blender. Thankfully, there’s all kinds of amazing tutorials for Unity game creation which you can find here. https://unity3d.com/learn/tutorials

Unity is fantastic because you can just drag and drop different prefabs into the scene and then easily manipulate them from there. Unfortunately for me I decided that I’d done enough work with game engines such as Hammer and RPG Maker 2000 that I didn’t need to look at tutorials before diving in. This would come back to bite me in the butt later on, but for the moment I began to construct a unique looking building from prefabs included in the asset store pack we were using.

This is Santa’s house, or at least, the very beginnings of it. Things just weren’t lining up, however, and I just could not make the seams between walls, floor, and roof, look like a complete house. This was probably 2 days of 2-4 hours of work to get to this point for me, because nothing was working in the drag-and-drop fashion I’d been expecting.

After some major googling, I finally realized it was because I was shaping and transforming the objects themselves, which kept changing which directiong “backwards” and “forwards” were. I soon learned that this was because I needed to parent all the objects to empty objects in the scene so I always had a consistent translation along the game plane. I could have saved myself 4-8 hours of work if I had just known this one simple thing. So, that’s when I started actually reading and watching basic tutorials.

And shortly thereafter, I had a pretty decent looking Santa House which would be located behind the player as this would be the location the player would defend from the evil snowmen. Being a VR game, I had to constantly check and recheck the scale of everything from the path up to the house, the height of the railings, the door, and trees. This meant lots of scaling, launching the game, putting the VR headset to my face, checking proportions, and going back to tweak and repeat the process all over again. As awesome as the building looked, however, it would honestly not be noticed by players due to it being directly behind the player but I felt very proud having created it. For this reason, we decided to make the in-game holiday music play directionally, as if it were coming from Santa’s house. This gave the building a sort of presence for the player so that even though they wouldn’t be seeing it very much, they would know it was there at all times.

Now it was time to move on to the Christmas Village that the player would actually spend most of their time seeing.

Hierarchies! Holy geeze these things are important. Having to create empty objects parented to each asset prefab meant I had twice the number of objects visible in the hierarchy than in the game. It was really hard to see what was what just by glancing at the hierarchy, so it was time to put everything together into neat little groups for organization as the level was now coming together.

This was the essence of the level that I had in mind from the very first day we thought of making this game. I had most of the idea plotted out in my head roughly, but we still didn’t have lighting, or a skybox, enemies, or music. I was actually a little depressed at this point because I’d spent so much time in Blender and Unity and I still didn’t really feel like I was actually making a game. It just didn’t feel like a game, because, well, there was literally no game there.

I spent a while looking for skyboxes that might help imbue the village with a little Christmas Cheer, and the above image is what I submitted to James for him to work on lighting and getting enemy pathing figured out. Like I said, I was pretty bummed out that night and went to bed a little frustrated and wondering if we would ever actually make this thing or if I was in way over my head. Then I woke up the next day to a little early Christmas miracle…

Lighting! James had pulled an all nighter tweaking and compiling the lighting. When I saw the game the next morning, I literally jumped out of my computer chair in excitement. It looked like a real game! And it now had that dark, warm, North Pole feeling I had been yearning to actually see within the game. Seeing the lighting was the very first time I actually felt like I was truly designing a real game.

We started working on enemy pathing and it was honestly a bit of a headache. The biggest issue was the red highlighted squares would spawn enemies that the player often couldn’t see due to the limited field of view innate in VR headsets. Your peripheral vision is completely gone and it would make for many instant losses for the player without them knowing what got them. The easiest solution was implementing a sound for the enemy as they approached the player. In this way, the player could hear when and if they were being snuck up on by the evil snowball throwing snowmen.

Remember that snazzy logo I made in Blender? Well, there it is, looking all fancy and awesome in front of the player! This was a real “wow” moment for me because I could see the text, the little tree, and the lightbulb all together actually within the scene and this was the first time I got to see how it looked within the actual game world and it looked great. We certainly had a game now, and we had a logo and name, and a simple UI to get started with.

This shows you the tree surroundings I created around the village which helped to occlude the skybox from the player and make the village seem more cozy as well. It really looks like a quaint little village there, and it was very exciting to be able to see the whole thing had come together as a cohesive idea from all the disparate parts.

It just wouldn’t be Christmas without snow, now would it? Loading into the game with the lighting, the holiday music coming from Santa’s house, the logo before you, and the snow falling down all around was the finishing touches needed to get the game into its final form. We had a game, and it felt like a game, and not just any game, it felt like our game, the one that only existed in our heads for weeks prior.

Thank you all so much for following this Dev Log. There’s so much more to say about this, including the challenges of playtesting with non-gamers who are new to VR, but I think that I have enough to say about it that if I have the time I’ll make that its own post and topic with fewer pictures.

Stay tuned because next week we’re showing off the very first images of our next VR game, a linear, story based VR game, a much larger and more daunting project compared to Holiday Hero.
